Part 1 — What Is Nonlinearity?¶

We begin by stepping beyond the assumptions of ordinary linear structural analysis.

Review: What Linear Analysis Assumes¶

In the usual linear structural analysis, we rely on two key assumptions:

  • Geometric linearity: deformations are small enough that equilibrium can be written on the undeformed geometry
  • Material linearity: stress is proportional to strain, so the material remains linearly elastic

In its simplest form, linear structural analysis gives a relation such as

$$ \mathbf{K}\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f} $$

where:

  • $\mathbf{K}$ is constant
  • response is proportional to load
  • superposition holds

This is often adequate for many structures under ordinary service-load conditions.

A Simple Mathematical View¶

In a linear structural problem, the applied load and the response are proportional.

For a single degree of freedom spring:

$$ f = ku $$

where:

  • $f$ = applied force
  • $u$ = displacement
  • $k$ = stiffness

If $k$ is constant, then doubling the load doubles the displacement:

$$ 2f = k \times 2u $$

So the force-displacement curve is a straight line.

In a nonlinear problem, that proportional relationship no longer holds.

The response may instead look like.

$$ f = f(u) $$

which means the force is some nonlinear function of displacement.

For example, the slope of a menber may change as the structure deforms, so the stiffness is no longer constant:

$$ k_{\text{tangent}} = \frac{df}{du} $$

At the structural level, we usually write the problem in terms of equilibrium between external and internal forces:

$$ \mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{ext}} - \mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{int}}(\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{0} $$

Here:

  • $\mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{ext}}$ is the applied load vector
  • $\mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{int}}(\mathbf{u})$ is the internal resisting force vector
  • $\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u})$ is the residual, or out-of-balance force

So in a nonlinear analysis, the goal is to find the displacement state $\mathbf{u}$ for which the structure is in equilibrium.

This is harder than the linear case because:

  • internal force depends on the current displacement
  • stiffness may change as the structure deforms
  • the solution usually must be found iteratively

Why Nonlinear Analysis Is Needed¶

As loads increase, or as deformations become significant, the assumptions of linear analysis begin to break down.

A structure may become nonlinear because:

  • its geometry changes enough during deformation to affect equilibrium and internal force
  • its material behavior changes, for example through yielding, cracking, or inelastic response

Then the structural response is no longer a straight-line relation between load and displacement.

Key idea: in a nonlinear problem, the structure you are solving for also changes while you solve it.

What Changes in Nonlinear Analysis?¶

In nonlinear analysis:

  • stiffness may change during loading
  • equilibrium may be written on the current or updated configuration
  • the load-displacement path may curve, soften, or stiffen
  • the response may involve instability, bifurcation, limit points, or post-critical behavior

So instead of solving once with a fixed stiffness matrix, we usually solve iteratively.

Why This Matters in Structural Engineering¶

Linear analysis is often very useful, but it has inherent limitations.

It may not capture:

  • loss of stiffness caused by large deformations
  • second-order effects such as $P$-$\Delta$ and $P$-$\delta$
  • instability phenomena such as buckling
  • inelastic material behavior and strength degradation

So nonlinear analysis becomes important when the goal is not only to compute forces and displacements under service conditions, but also to understand:

  • ultimate strength
  • stability
  • large-deformation behavior
  • failure mechanisms

Part 2 — Types of Nonlinearity¶

Material Nonlinearity¶

One major source of structural nonlinearity is material nonlinearity.

The constitutive law is nonlinear, so the relationship between stress and strain is no longer a straight line.

For a linear elastic material, we write

$$ \sigma = E\varepsilon $$

where the slope $E$ is constant.

For a nonlinear material, the stress is some more general function of strain:

$$ \sigma = \sigma(\varepsilon) $$

So the material stiffness is no longer constant. Instead, the slope changes as the material deforms:

$$ E_t = \frac{d\sigma}{d\varepsilon} $$

Examples of Material Nonlinearity¶

Material nonlinearity arises when the stress--strain law is not linear elastic.

Typical examples include:

  • yielding of steel
  • cracking of reinforced concrete
  • creep or time-dependent deformation
  • nonlinear interaction of axial force, bending, shear, and torsion

Conceptually:

  • the material stiffness changes as loading proceeds
  • unloading and reloading may follow different paths
  • the load-displacement curve can become history-dependent
For a linear elastic material, the slope is constant. For a nonlinear material, the tangent stiffness changes along the response path.

Geometric Nonlinearity¶

The second major source is geometric nonlinearity.

The deformation is large enough that the changing geometry affects equilibrium, compatibility, and internal force.

In a linear analysis, we treat the structure as if its geometry does not change significantly during loading, so the stiffness is taken as fixed.

In a geometrically nonlinear analysis, the current deformed shape affects the response, so the stiffness depends on displacement:

$$ \mathbf{K} = \mathbf{K}(\mathbf{u}) $$

Equivalently, the internal resisting force depends on the current configuration:

$$ \mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{int}} = \mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{int}}(\mathbf{u}) $$

Examples of Geometric Nonlinearity¶

Geometric nonlinearity arises when deformations become large enough that the current geometry cannot be ignored.

Typical examples include:

  • $P$-$\Delta$ effect: gravity load acting through a lateral story displacement
  • $P$-$\delta$ effect: axial force interacting with member curvature
  • cables, membranes, or very flexible trusses whose stiffness depends strongly on shape
  • structures undergoing large displacement before reaching material failure

Conceptually:

  • equilibrium must be written on the deformed configuration
  • internal force depends on both displacement and current axial state
  • stiffness becomes configuration-dependent

Combined Nonlinear Effects¶

In many realistic structural problems, the two types of nonlinearity occur together.

Examples include:

  • a steel frame with both yielding and $P$-$\Delta$ effects
  • reinforced concrete members with cracking and large deflections
  • frames where connection deformation, panel-zone deformation, and stability effects all interact

A useful way to think about it is:

  • material nonlinearity changes the constitutive law
  • geometric nonlinearity changes the equilibrium problem

In a first introduction, it is common to isolate one source of nonlinearity at a time.

Part 3 — Levels of Analysis¶

Why We Talk About Levels of Analysis¶

It is rarely practical to model every source of nonlinearity in full detail.

So structural analysis is often organized into levels of analysis, where each level captures more of the true behavior than the one before it.

These levels differ in:

  • what assumptions are retained
  • what sources of nonlinearity are included
  • what kinds of failure or instability they can detect
  • the computational effort required

Each higher level is not just "more complicated" — it is answering a different question about the structure.

First-Order Elastic Analysis¶

This is the standard linear analysis we have used so far.

Assumptions

  • equilibrium written on the undeformed structure
  • material remains linearly elastic
  • stiffness remains constant

What it misses

  • geometric nonlinear effects
  • material nonlinear effects
  • instability behavior

$$ \mathbf{K}\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f} $$

Second-Order Elastic Analysis¶

This level includes the effects of deformation on equilibrium while still assuming elastic material behavior.

So it can represent:

  • finite displacement effects
  • $P$-$\Delta$ effects
  • $P$-$\delta$ effects
  • some aspects of stability and load amplification

But it still does not represent yielding, cracking, or other material nonlinearity.

Symbolically, the problem begins to look like

$$ \mathbf{K}(\mathbf{u}) \times \mathbf{u} = \mathbf{f} $$

or, more carefully, as a sequence of updated equilibrium states.

Several possible modes of nonlinear elastic behavior are shown.

  • bifurcation of the load path into an alternative branch
  • gradual softening that culminates in an elastic instability at a limit point
  • stiffness changes that may lead to either softening or stiffening behavior

First-Order Inelastic and Second-Order Inelastic Analysis¶

More advanced levels of analysis include material nonlinearity, with or without geometric nonlinearity.

First-order inelastic

  • includes material nonlinearity
  • keeps equilibrium on the undeformed geometry
  • can represent plastic response, but not geometric nonlinear instability effects

Second-order inelastic

  • includes both material and geometric nonlinearity
  • is the most general of these common levels
  • can represent strength loss, stability limits, and strongly nonlinear response

Much more involved than what we need for our immediate goals.

Summary of the Levels of Analysis¶

Level of analysis Geometric nonlinearity? Material nonlinearity? Typical use
First-order elastic No No Basic service-load response
Second-order elastic Yes No Large-displacement elastic response, stability effects
First-order inelastic No Yes Inelastic material response without geometric effects
Second-order inelastic Yes Yes Most general response including strength and stability

Part 4 — Scope for This Course¶

What We Will Focus On¶

In CEE6501, we will not attempt a general treatment of all nonlinear structural analysis.

Instead, we will focus on:

  • geometric nonlinearity
  • axial elements only
  • bar and joint frameworks (trusses, space frames, cable nets, etc.)
  • initially in 2D plane trusses

So the goal is not a full nonlinear analysis of general frames or continuous surfaces.

The goal is to understand the basic concepts of geometrically nonlinear analysis for axial-member structures whose stiffness and equilibrium depend strongly on their deformed shape.

Why This Narrower Scope Makes Sense¶

This narrower focus connects directly to form finding, which is fundamentally an inverse problem:

  • instead of asking for the response of a known structure
  • we ask what structural form results from a given set of inputs, constraints, and force patterns

That requires the structure to change shape until forces come into equilibrium, so it is naturally formulated as a geometrically nonlinear problem.

That is exactly the setting for methods such as:

  • force density methods (next lecture)
  • dynamic relaxation (last lecture)

So before we can find shapes that are in equilibrium, we first need a basic understanding of geometric nonlinearity.

Part 5 — Basic Concept of Geometrically Nonlinear Analysis¶

The Example Structure¶

To illustrate the basic concept of geometrically nonlinear analysis, we consider a simple symmetric two-member plane truss made of a linearly elastic material.

The structure is loaded by a vertical load $P$ at the top joint, so the motion is described by a single degree of freedom:

$$ \delta = \text{vertical displacement of joint 2} $$

Because the structure is symmetric:

  • both members have the same undeformed length $L$
  • both members have the same area $A$
  • both members have the same angle $\theta$ in the undeformed configuration
  • both members carry the same axial force magnitude $Q$

This very simple system is enough to show the essential difference between linear and geometrically nonlinear analysis.

Linear Analysis: Small-Displacement Kinematics¶

In linear analysis, the displacement $\delta$ is assumed to be small enough that the member axial deformation can be approximated from the undeformed geometry.

For each member,

$$ u \approx \delta \sin\theta \tag{1} $$

so the axial strain is

$$ \varepsilon = \frac{u}{L} \approx \left(\frac{\sin\theta}{L}\right)\delta \tag{2} $$

This is a linear relation between strain and joint displacement.

The key approximation is that the member direction is taken from the original configuration, not the deformed one ($L = L_0$)

Linear Analysis: Equilibrium on the Undeformed Geometry¶

In linear analysis, the equilibrium equations are also written on the undeformed geometry.

At the loaded top joint, vertical equilibrium gives

$$ Q \approx \frac{P}{2\sin\theta} \tag{3} $$

and therefore the axial stress is

$$ \sigma = \frac{Q}{A} \approx \frac{P}{2A\sin\theta} \tag{4} $$

For a linearly elastic material,

$$ \sigma = E\varepsilon \tag{5} $$

Combining the linear expressions for stress, Eq. (4), and strain, Eq. (2), gives the linear load-displacement relation:

$$ P \approx \underbrace{\left(\frac{2EA\sin^2\theta}{L}\right)}_{\text{stiffness term}} \delta \tag{6} $$

So in the linear model, the structure behaves like a spring with constant stiffness:

$$ k = \frac{2EA\sin^2\theta}{L} $$

Geometrically Nonlinear Analysis: Use the Deformed Geometry¶

We do not assume the displacement is small.

$$ \bar{L} = \sqrt{(L\cos\theta)^2 + (L\sin\theta - \delta)^2} = L\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta} \tag{7} $$

$$ \sin\bar{\theta} = \frac{L\sin\theta-\delta}{\bar{L}} = \frac{\sin\theta-\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)} {\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta}} \tag{8} $$

Exact Axial Deformation and Strain¶

The first key difference from linear analysis is in the kinematics. The member deformation is computed from the actual deformed length.

The exact axial deformation is therefore

$$ u = \bar{L} - L $$

Substituting the expression for the deformed member length from Eq. (7) gives

$$ u = L\left[ \sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta} -1 \right] \tag{9} $$

and the corresponding axial strain becomes

$$ \varepsilon = \frac{u}{L} = \sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta} -1 \tag{10} $$

So, unlike the linear case, both axial deformation and strain are now nonlinear functions of the joint displacement $\delta$.

Equilibrium Must Also Be Written on the Deformed Geometry¶

The second key difference from linear analysis is in the equilibrium equations since when the current member angle changes, so too does the vertical equilibrium relation.

vertical equilibrium at the top joint

$$ Q = \frac{P}{2\sin\bar{\theta}} \tag{11} $$

So unlike the linear case, the internal member force is not related to $P$ through the original angle $\theta$, but through the current angle $\bar{\theta}$.

Substituting the expression for the deformed angle from Eq. (8) gives

$$ Q = \frac{P\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta}} {2\left[\sin\theta-\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\right]} $$

which yields the expression for member axial stress as

$$ \sigma = \frac{Q}{A} = \frac{P\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta}} {2A\left[\sin\theta-\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\right]} \tag{12} $$

Finally, by substituting the expressions for strain, Eq. (10), and stress, Eq. (12), into the linear stress-strain relationship $\sigma = E\varepsilon$, we obtain the nonlinear load-displacement relation for the truss:

$$ P = 2EA\left[\sin\theta-\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\right] \left[ \frac{1-\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta}} {\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta}} \right] \tag{13} $$

Result: A Nonlinear Load-Displacement Relation¶

After combining:

  • the exact deformed geometry
  • the exact strain expression
  • Hooke's law, $\sigma = E\varepsilon$
  • and equilibrium on the deformed configuration

we obtain a nonlinear relation between load $P$ and displacement $\delta$.

Symbolically, the important point is:

$$ P = P(\delta) $$

not

$$ P = k\delta $$

with constant stiffness.

So even with a linearly elastic material, the structural response becomes nonlinear because the geometry changes.

Summary — Two Key Differences¶

Kinematics

In linear analysis,

$$ u \approx \delta \sin\theta $$

In geometrically nonlinear analysis,

$$ u = \bar{L} - L $$

So the member deformation is based on the exact deformed geometry, not a small-displacement approximation.

Equilibrium

In linear analysis, equilibrium is written on the undeformed geometry.

In geometrically nonlinear analysis, equilibrium is written on the deformed geometry.

So both the deformation and the equilibrium equations depend on the current shape of the structure.

delta = np.arange(0, 4000, 5)  # mm

# Linear model
P_lin = (2 * E * A * np.sin(theta)**2 / L) * delta

# Geometrically nonlinear model from the exact truss geometry
x = delta / L
root = np.sqrt(1 + x**2 - 2*x*np.sin(theta))
P_geo = 2 * E * A * (np.sin(theta) - x) * (1 - root) / root

The geometrically nonlinear model is based on the exact relation

$$ P = 2EA\left[\sin\theta-\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\right] \left[ \frac{1-\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta}} {\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)^2-2\left(\frac{\delta}{L}\right)\sin\theta}} \right] \tag{13} $$

Interpreting the Response Curve¶

From the plot, we observe:

  • the linear solution increases indefinitely with constant slope
  • the geometrically nonlinear solution gradually softens as displacement increases
  • the two predictions agree at small displacement, then diverge more and more as deformation grows

This means the linear approximation becomes progressively less accurate as the geometry change becomes significant.

Critical Point and Tangent Stiffness¶

The slope of the nonlinear response curve is the tangent stiffness.

At its peak the tangent stiffness becomes zero:

$$ \frac{dP}{d\delta} = 0 $$

When it reaches 0, the structure has reached a critical or limit point.

For the shallow two-member truss, the corresponding critical displacement is

$$ \delta_{cr} = L\left[\sin\theta - \cos\theta\sqrt{\frac{1}{(\cos\theta)^{2/3}} - 1}\right] \tag{14} $$

Substituting $\delta_{cr}$ into Eq. (13) gives the critical load $P_{cr}$.

This is the point where the structure loses its ability to resist any further increase in load along the same equilibrium path.

delta_cr = L * (np.sin(theta) - np.cos(theta) * np.sqrt(1 / (np.cos(theta)**(2/3)) - 1))

x_cr = delta_cr / L
root_cr = np.sqrt(1 + x_cr**2 - 2 * x_cr * np.sin(theta))
P_cr = 2 * E * A * (np.sin(theta) - x_cr) * (1 - root_cr) / root_cr

Physical Meaning of the Critical Point¶

The critical point marks the end of stable load increase along the original equilibrium branch.

Before that point:

  • additional displacement requires additional load
  • the structure still has positive tangent stiffness

At the critical point:

  • the tangent stiffness becomes zero
  • the response curve reaches a peak
  • the structure is on the verge of instability

Snap-Through Instability¶

Beyond the critical point, a further increase in load cannot be sustained on the same branch.

Instead, the truss may undergo snap-through, moving suddenly to a very different equilibrium configuration.

This is important because:

  • large changes in shape can occur suddenly
  • the linear model gives no warning of this behavior

Why This Example Matters¶

This example captures the essential message of geometric nonlinearity:

  • the material may remain linearly elastic
  • but the structural response can still be highly nonlinear
  • the source of the nonlinearity is the changing geometry

That is why geometric nonlinear analysis is so important for flexible structures and for later topics such as equilibrium shape finding and form finding.
